# Catio v2 — DIY Apparatus Catalog

**Scope:** interior fit-out only — the stuff that goes *inside* the envelope. Structure, footprint, and layout belong to the other build track; this catalog is what gets mounted, stacked, and hung once the bones exist. Everything here is buildable from lumberyard stock plus a hardware run. No prefab cat furniture.

**Design users:**
- **Hobie** — ~20 lb, 6 yrs, high prey drive, completes full hunt sequences, heat-seeking. Governs *load ratings, shelf depth, and door hole sizes*. Treat him as a structural consideration.
- **Zoey** — ~11 lb, 14.5 yrs, fast and vital *today*, but the build should be ready for 16-year-old Zoey. Governs *route design*: everything reachable by at least one low-effort path.

**Climate:** Hudson Valley four-season. Freeze/thaw, snow load on anything horizontal, humid summers, real winter wind. Materials section at the bottom addresses all of it.

**V2 envelope (from the model, draft r5):** 88" E–W × 78" N–S, 80" panels, roof TBD (flat wire ceiling at 80" is the leading candidate). The north wall is the house side — the sectional runs its full length and the cat-flap exits onto the sofa back (~29" high × ~84" long: effectively a pre-built low catwalk, so routes should key off it). Chaise on the west wall; the apparatus wall is the east face + SE corner. Panel rails sit at ~24.75" / 55.25" / 79.25" and are the natural shelf datums — but the 30.5" gap between the mid rails exceeds hop spec, so each climbing bay needs one intermediate step. Consequences for items below: the skywalk (§1.2) wants the 55.25" rail datum (~24" headroom under an 80" ceiling); the branch tree (§1.3) needs the roof call or a freestanding/knee-braced design; the exercise wheel (§4.7) is a serious floor-space negotiation in this footprint — genuinely v2.1 material.

## Design constants — pin these before anything gets mounted

| Spec | Number | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Shelf/perch depth | 10" min; 11.25" (1x12 cedar) ideal | Hobie needs real estate to turn around and loaf |
| Perch length | 16–24"; landings ≥ 12x18" | Full-body lounging, not tightrope perching |
| Vertical hop spacing | 12–16" standard; **9–12" on the senior route** | Easy repeated hops, not maximal athletic ones |
| Horizontal gap between platforms | 12–16" easy; **24" is a hard limit, not a default** | Cats *can* jump farther; they won't want to daily |
| Catwalk width | 10–12"; widen to 14"+ at passing points | Two cats meeting on a highway need a pull-out |
| Headroom above top runs | 14"+ to roof/mesh | A cat should be able to sit upright on the skywalk |
| Ramp slope | **18–20° for the senior route** (~3:1 run:rise); ≤30° elsewhere | 18–20° is the range used for senior/rehab pet ramps |
| Ramp cleats | Every ~4" (≈ Zoey's front-to-rear paw stance) | Rest-and-grip points on the climb |
| Cubby door hole | 6.5–7" diameter | Hobie clearance |
| Design load, every perch | 50 lb static minimum | A 20-lb cat landing hard is a multiple of body weight — plus snow, plus you grabbing it for balance |
| Route redundancy | Every high point: ≥2 ways on/off, one low-effort | No single-entry traps; critical in a two-cat household |

Two route-design principles worth internalizing, both from people who've watched cats refuse expensive furniture: **the higher the platform, the *easier* the moves to reach it should be** (hesitation compounds with height), and **a high perch with only one exit is a trap** — cats avoid spots where another cat can pin them.

## Category 1 — Vertical & traverse

### 1.1 Cedar shelf runs (the workhorse)
Staggered switchback of 1x10/1x12 tight-knot cedar shelves climbing a wall. This is the backbone everything else connects to.

- **Build:** Lag heavy shelf brackets or French cleats into framing (never just mesh framing strips — into real studs/posts). 2 brackets per shelf up to ~24", 3 beyond.
- **Layout:** Alternate left/right so the climb zigzags; keep vertical hops in-spec per route (standard vs. senior).
- **Detail worth doing:** a ¾" front lip on shelves above ~4 ft — footing confidence and stops naps from becoming slides.

### 1.2 Perimeter skywalk (the marquee feature)
A continuous elevated run around the top perimeter — the single highest-value build in any catio. Cats patrol; give them a patrol route that circumnavigates the territory without ever descending.

- **Build:** 2x10/2x12 or doubled 5/4x6 cedar decking on a ledger + brackets into the top framing, every 24–32".
- **Corners:** land them on 16x16" corner platforms — corners are where cats stop and survey, so make corners generous.
- **Passing bulges:** 14"+ wide sections every 6–8 ft so Hobie and Zoey can negotiate right-of-way without a standoff.
- **Headroom:** keep 14"+ below the roof/mesh so the walk is also a sit.

### 1.3 Real-branch climbing tree
A 4–6" diameter hardwood limb — black locust if you can find it (extremely rot-resistant and everywhere in Ulster County), otherwise oak/maple/apple — mounted floor to top plate as the vertical highway. Bark on. This is simultaneously the best climbing structure and the best scratcher in the whole build, and it's free.

- **Build:** Flatten the ends with a chainsaw/circular saw, anchor with heavy angle brackets top and bottom (lag through the branch), angle it 10–20° off vertical so it's climbable rather than shimmy-able.
- **Prep:** knock off loose bark, check for rot/insect galleries, let it dry a few weeks if fresh-cut. Radial checking (cracking) as it dries is normal and fine.
- **Avoid:** cherry and walnut limbs; stick to the safe-list species above.

### 1.4 Stump forest
Three or four log rounds at staggered heights (e.g., 12" / 20" / 28") clustered as natural stairs, scratch stations, and scent posts. Firewood-round diameter (10"+) so the tops are sittable. In your area these are literally free — a couple of rounds off someone's downed oak and you have sculpture.

- **Build:** Basically none — level the tops, set on pavers or a gravel bed so end grain isn't wicking ground moisture, screw a hidden bracket to the deck/frame if any of them wobble.
- **Lifespan:** they'll slowly weather and get gloriously shredded. That's the point. Replace in a few years.

### 1.5 Senior switchback ramp — the Zoey route
A dedicated cleated ramp from floor to mid-height (then easy 9–12" hops the rest of the way), so every destination in the catio remains reachable when jumping stops being fun. Build it now, while she doesn't need it — cats learn routes when they're able and keep using them when they're not.

- **Specs:** 10" wide, 18–20° slope (that's under a 4/12 pitch — gentler than your roof), cleats every 4", low 1x2 side rails (older cats use edge contact as a proprioception cue), level landing platform at the top.
- **Surface:** cleats + bare cedar is enough; add rubber stair-tread strip between cleats if it ever sees ice.

### 1.6 Spiral stair post (optional flourish)
Small cedar treads radiating around a 4x4 post, ~10–12" rise per tread, quarter-turn spacing. Compact vertical circulation for a corner where a full shelf run won't fit. Looks fantastic; entirely optional.

## Category 2 — Bridges & tunnels

### 2.1 Slat-and-rope suspension bridge
Cedar slats (1x4, ~14" long) threaded on four runs of 3/8" sisal or manila, spanning between two platforms. The slight sway is a feature — vestibular enrichment, and cats visibly enjoy the mastery of it.

- **Build:** Drill slats near corners, thread rope, knot between each slat to fix spacing (~1" gaps), terminate on heavy eye bolts with thimbles. Keep the span modest (3–5 ft) and the sag shallow.
- **Rule:** wobbly crossings are a *bonus* route, never the *only* route. Zoey gets a solid alternative.
- **Rope safety:** taut runs, no loose loops or dangling tails anywhere a cat plays unobserved.

### 2.2 Solid plank bridge
The dignified crossing: a 2x10 with low 1x2 rails between platforms or skywalk segments. Boring to build, used fifty times a day. Where a gap exceeds the 24" limit, this is the fix.

### 2.3 Box tunnel (ground or elevated)
An open-ended cedar/ply box, ~10x10" interior, 24–48" long — on the floor as a hide-and-ambush station, or elevated as an enclosed segment of the shelf network. Enclosed hiding spots measurably reduce stress in cats, and for a cat like Hobie who runs complete hunt sequences, a tunnel is ambush architecture.

- **Detail:** cut 2–3 paw-sized peek/pounce ports along the sides. This turns a box into a game.
- **Elevated version:** it's just a shelf with walls and a roof — same mounting rules, mind the added weight.

## Category 3 — Lounging & shelter

### 3.1 Mesh sling hammocks
Outdoor sling-chair mesh (Phifertex/Textilene — the vinyl-coated stuff patio furniture uses) stretched on a cedar frame or across a corner. Drains instantly, dries fast, shrugs off claws, and gives that suspended give cats love.

- **Build (corner triangle):** hem or double-fold the mesh edges, set grommets, mount on stainless eye bolts + carabiners at three points. Carabiners mean it unclips for hosing off or winter storage in ten seconds.
- **Build (framed):** staple/screw mesh under a 2x2 cedar frame with fender washers so it can't tear through; frame drops into cleats on the wall.
- **Size:** 18x18" minimum; go 22–24" for a Hobie-rated model. Mount 12–24" up with an easy hop or step access.
- **Skip cotton/canvas duck:** mildews and stays wet. Sunbrella is the acceptable fabric alternative.

### 3.2 Corner triangle perches
The cheapest useful thing in this document: ¾" exterior-grade plywood triangles (18–20" legs) on cleats in corners, edges eased and sealed. Scatter them at various heights as rest stops between the bigger apparatus. Fifteen minutes each.

### 3.3 Sun deck
One deliberately oversized platform (24x18"+) placed wherever the low winter sun actually lands — south/southeast exposure, sited by observation, not guesswork. In January this will be the most contested square footage in the catio. A black cat is a solar collector; give him a panel.

- **Detail:** slight tilt (¼" per foot) so it sheds rain, front lip so the melted cat doesn't ooze off.

### 3.4 Weatherproof cubby / winter cabin
An enclosed box that's a shaded den in summer and a genuinely warm shelter in winter.

- **Base spec:** ~20x20" interior floor, 16" interior height (Hobie-rated), 6.5–7" door hole faced away from prevailing wind, floor raised 4"+ off the deck, hinged sloped roof for cleaning, drainage gap at the floor seam.
- **Winter upgrade:** double-wall it — 1" rigid foam sandwiched between ply skins, *fully enclosed* so no foam is ever chewable/scratchable. Bed with straw, not blankets or hay (straw sheds moisture; fabric holds it and goes cold).
- **Two cats, one rule:** if they both take to it, build a second. Prime shelter is not a shareable resource in most multi-cat households.

### 3.5 Grass day-bed
A planter wide enough to *nap in* (18"+ across, a half whiskey barrel or a built cedar box), sown with oat/wheat cat grass. It's a snack and a bed in one, and live turf runs measurably cooler than everything around it in summer — evaporative cooling, free of charge.

- **Maintenance:** grass gets loved to death; keep two trays and rotate one indoors to regrow.

## Category 4 — Play & senses

### 4.1 Scratching infrastructure
Three tiers, cheapest first:

- **Bark-on log section** — a 6–10" diameter log, mounted at a 45° lean or laid horizontal. The genuinely best scratcher that exists; also a scent-marking station, which matters in a two-cat territory.
- **Sisal-wrapped 4x4** — 32–36" tall minimum (a full vertical stretch for a big cat), wrapped in 3/8" sisal. Screw-terminate the rope ends rather than gluing so the wrap is replaceable when it weathers.
- **Horizontal scratch plank** — some cats split the vote on vertical vs. horizontal; a sisal- or bark-faced plank on the floor covers the other party.

### 4.2 Toy arms
Springy wand mounts so solo play happens without you: a spring-steel or fiberglass rod (driveway-marker stock works) clamped to a post, toy on a short leader off the tip. Every batted hit reloads itself.

- **Printer tie-in:** mounting clips, quick-release toy hitches, and swap-able arm bases are ideal A1 fodder — print in **PETG or ASA**, not PLA, which sags and chalks in summer sun. A modular toy-arm socket system is exactly the kind of overkill this project deserves.
- **Safety:** short leaders (< 8"), no loops, nothing elastic enough to snap back into an eye.

### 4.3 Foraging log
A log or thick plank drilled with 1–1.5" pockets that hold treats or kibble — paw-fishing puzzle, zero moving parts.

- **⚠ The wildlife rule (non-negotiable in the Catskills):** nothing edible stays in the catio unattended, ever. Food scent recruits raccoons first and black bears eventually, and both will test mesh. The foraging log is a *supervised-session* toy that lives indoors.

### 4.4 Planter rail
A narrow planter box along one wall for catnip, valerian, and rotating cat grass — a scent garden at cat height.

- **Hard no-list anywhere in or near the catio:** lilies (all types — genuinely deadly to cats, even pollen), azalea, foxglove, morning glory. When in doubt, check ASPCA's plant list before anything goes in soil.

### 4.5 Cat TV — feeder pole
A bird feeder on a pole sited *outside* the catio, 6–10 ft from the mesh, in the sightline of a good perch. Endless programming; the birds habituate to enclosed cats quickly at that distance.

- **Local reality check:** NYSDEC advises pulling feeders for the warm months (roughly April–November) in bear country, which is us. This is winter programming — which is conveniently when the catio needs it most.

### 4.6 Dig box
A low tray of coarse play sand — digging is real enrichment that indoor cats rarely get. Honest caveat: there's a coin-flip chance it gets annexed as a latrine. Build it as a dumpable tray, not a fixture, and demote it without sentiment if it turns.

### 4.7 Exercise wheel (boss-level build)
A 42–48" diameter running wheel: two plywood rings, a bent-luan running band, cork or EVA foam tread, spinning on polyurethane roller wheels set in a cradle base. This is a genuine multi-weekend project with real fabrication in it — but you have the shop skills, and an orange cat who still hits zoomie speeds at 14.5 is the exact demographic. File under "v2.1."

### 4.8 Water feature (optional)
Cats prefer moving water; a glazed pot with a small solar fountain pump makes a drinking station they'll actually use. The pump is the one bought component in this document — call it a rounding error. Dump and refresh often in summer (mosquitoes).

## Category 5 — Comfort & seasonal

### 5.1 Shade panel
Shade cloth (50–70%) or a solid panel over one section for July — a black cat in full sun has no chill mode. Sited opposite the sun deck so the catio always offers both ends of the thermal spectrum. Grommeted cloth on hooks = seasonal, removable.

### 5.2 Wind panels
Clear polycarbonate panels screwed over the mesh on the north/northwest face for winter — kills the prevailing wind without killing the view. Design as removable storm panels (quarter-turn fasteners or screws into permanent battens) that come off in April.

### 5.3 Traction & wear surfaces
Ranked for outdoor durability: **bare cedar** (fine as-is for most surfaces) → **kerf grooves** (crosswise saw kerfs, ⅛" deep every inch, on ramps and high runs — elegant, permanent, zero maintenance) → **rubber stair-tread strips** (screwed, replaceable, for ice-prone spots). Outdoor carpet only on removable panels — glued-down carpet holds water and composts itself within two seasons out here.

## Materials cheat sheet — Hudson Valley four-season edition

**Wood, contact surfaces (anything cats touch):** tight-knot cedar for shelves and runs; ¾" exterior-grade (AC/BC) plywood for platforms and boxes with all cut edges sealed. Cedar's natural oils resist rot and bugs with zero chemistry.

**Wood, structure:** modern pressure-treated (ACQ/copper azole, post-2003 — no arsenic) is broadly considered fine and is the right call for anything near grade. That said, most catio builders still keep PT away from surfaces cats habitually scratch, chew, or lick — cedar is cheap insurance there, so spend the extra few bucks where paws and tongues live.

**Finish:** raw cedar is a legitimate finish — it grays, it doesn't rot. Where you do seal (horizontal wear surfaces, ply edges), use a water-based exterior poly or pet-safe deck sealer, low-VOC, and give it a genuine multi-day cure with airflow before the cats regain access. Cure time is the whole safety story with finishes.

**Hardware:** exterior-coated or stainless screws everywhere; no nails in anything a cat lands on. Fender washers wherever fabric or mesh is fastened. Stainless eye bolts + carabiners for anything that should come down for washing or winter.

**Fabric:** Phifertex/Textilene sling mesh first, Sunbrella second, cotton never.

**Rope:** 3/8" natural sisal for wraps and bridges; inspect seasonally, rewrap when fuzzed to death. Skip synthetic rope on scratch surfaces — shredded poly strands are an ingestion problem.

**Printed parts:** PETG or ASA only. PLA is a summer casualty.

## The Zoey layer — senior-proofing, summarized

1. **One continuous low-effort route** from floor to the highest perch: ramp + 9–12" steps, no segment requiring a real jump.
2. **No single-exit perches up high.** Every destination has two ways off.
3. **Lips and rails** on anything above 4 ft; grippier surfaces the higher you go.
4. **Landings** top and bottom of every ramp — level staging, no launching from an incline.
5. **Reassess yearly.** The route that's trivially easy at 14.5 gets a fresh look at 16. The infrastructure above makes those adjustments trivial: add a cleat, add a step.

She'll ignore all of it and take the hard route for years. That's fine — Miss Zoey Genie Regina Pumpernickle Noveck Hotwagner will have a route befitting her titles ready when she wants it.

## Placement notes — handoff for the layout track

Not touching the model, per instructions — but these constraints belong in whoever's doing layout:

- **Sun-map before mounting anything.** South/southeast = winter sun deck; north/northwest = wind panels + summer shade. An hour of observation beats any assumption.
- **Skywalk needs 14"+ headroom** below roof/mesh, continuous around the perimeter, with corner landings.
- **Two routes to every destination**; passing bulges where routes cross.
- **At least one prime perch aimed at the yard action** (future feeder pole sightline).
- **Human circulation:** nothing in the door swing, nothing head-height in the walking path, bench/seating placed so lap access is on the cats' terms (it will be regardless).
- **All mounting into real framing** — apparatus loads never carried by mesh or furring strips.

## Suggested build order

**Phase 1 — Skeleton (one weekend):** shelf runs, senior ramp, corner triangles, one cubby. The catio is fully functional after this phase alone.

**Phase 2 — The good stuff:** perimeter skywalk, branch tree, stump forest, scratch log, solid bridge.

**Phase 3 — Luxuries:** hammocks, sun deck, grass day-bed, planter rail, suspension bridge, toy arms, wind/shade panels.

**Ongoing / DLC:** feeder pole (winter), dig box (probationary), water feature, and — when the itch strikes — the wheel.
